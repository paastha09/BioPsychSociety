About

Organized by the McMaster Biology & Psychology Society, the Neuroscience Society and the Mental Health Society, the NeuroXChange Conference (NXC) is a Canada-wide undergraduate research conference that brings together undergraduate students to showcase their research in the field of neuroscience. The conference provides the opportunity for undergraduate students to explore their interest in neuroscience, and encourages their ongoing growth in the field as they continue into graduate or professional studies. 

Any undergraduate students involved in neuroscience research at a university in Canada are welcome to present at the conference. Anyone interested in neuroscience research is welcome to attend.

Organizers

Who Can Attend?

Attendees

Anyone may attend, including undergraduate students, graduate students, and faculty.

The cost for attendees is $15 plus applicable fees. Refreshments, lunch, conference program, reusable NXC bag, and commemorative gifts will be provided under this price. Tickets must be purchased in advance by clicking the "Buy Ticket" button on the top right of the screen, or if you are using a mobile device, purchase tickets under the registration section .

Presentees

Students must be undergraduate students currently attending a Canadian University and must be involved or have previously taken part in the research project they are presenting.

The research project must be completed under the supervision of a faculty member at a university located within Canada. The student must obtain consent from their supervisor to present the research project. The presenter must ensure their research is in accordance with one of the research areas in neuroscience (see Research Areas).

The research must be consistent with McMaster University's Academic Integrity Guidelines.

For more information on prices, registration, and types of presentations you are able to do, visit the " Presenter Info" tab.

Benefits of Presenting

Honorariums will be awarded for the most outstanding poster presentations, as determined by a panel of undergraduate and graduate judges.

One honorarium will be awarded for the most outstanding oral presentation, as determined by a panel of undergraduate and graduate judges.

Multiple abstract publications in the McMaster Meducator Journal will be awarded to outstanding presentations, as determined by a panel of McMaster Meducator Executives. This year we are also pleased to invite Psynapse . The Psynapse: McMaster Undergraduate Journal of PNB is the first journal dedicated to publishing McMaster undergraduate research under the fields of psychology, neuroscience, and behaviour. With our inaugural issue published in 2019, the Psynapse provides a platform for students under the PNB department or taking PNB classes to disseminate their undergraduate research. The journal focuses on creating a literary community to ultimately enable meaningful academic discussions. A focus on effective science communication further allows the Psynapse to connect with the rest of McMaster student community. Written pieces found on the journal are clear and concise, ensuring that anyone, from a senior PNB student to a student in their first year, can understand the research they are reading.

Last Year's Keynote Speakers

Dr. Katherine Holshausen

Mental Health Keynote

Dr. Katherine Holshausen completed her Bachelor of Arts in Psychology at McMaster University, going on to complete her Master of Science and PhD in Clinical Psychology at Queen’s University.

Dr. Holshausen began working at St. Joseph’s Healthcare Hamilton in 2017. She is now a staff psychologist in the Borderline Personality Disorder Service and Research Director of the Community Psychiatry Clinic; she is also an Assistant Professor in the Department of Psychiatry and Behavioural Neuroscience at McMaster University.

Dr. Holshausen’s research focuses on program evaluation and examination of symptomatic and functional outcomes following psychotherapy, with a specific focus on emotion regulation, borderline personality disorder, psychosis, and trauma.

Dr. Nozomu Sato

Neuroscience Keynote

Dr. Nozomu Sato graduated from Medical School, Tokyo Medical and Dental University in 2000. After finishing his residency in Tokyo, he chose to pursue neurology as his specialty.

After five years of clinical training, he started his research on the molecular genetics of hereditary neurodegenerative disorders. He received Ph.D. for identifying the mutation for spinocerebellar ataxia 31 (SCA31) in 2009. While working as a clinical neurologist, he continued his research on the molecular pathogenesis of SCA31, and identified binding proteins to the pathogenic transcripts of SCA31. He also worked on genetic studies on ataxias of unknown cause, and contributed to the discovery of the first Japanese pedigrees of SCA34 and SCA42.

In May, 2018, he joined Dr Christopher E. Pearson’s Lab at The Hospital for Sick Children Research Institute, Toronto, hoping to develop effective treatment for neurodegenerative disorders caused by expansion of short tandem repeats.
